> Show that a full parenthesization of an $$n$$-element expression has exactly $$n - 1$$ pairs of parentheses.

A full parenthesization of an expression means that every binary operation is explicitly enclosed in parentheses. For example, with 4 elements $$A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4$$, one full parenthesization is $$((A_1 A_2)(A_3 A_4))$$, which has 3 pairs of parentheses.

Think of building a full parenthesization as constructing a binary tree: each internal node represents a multiplication operation, and each leaf represents a matrix. Every time we combine two subexpressions, we add one pair of parentheses around them.

We prove this by induction on $$n$$.

**Base case** ($$n = 1$$): A single element $$A_1$$ requires no parentheses since there are no operations to perform. We have $$n - 1 = 0$$ pairs of parentheses. Alternatively, the element itself can be viewed as being parenthesized as $$(A_1)$$, but by convention, a "full parenthesization" of a single element needs no parentheses.

**Inductive hypothesis**: Assume that for all $$k < n$$, a full parenthesization of a $$k$$-element expression has exactly $$k - 1$$ pairs of parentheses.

**Inductive step**: Consider a full parenthesization of an $$n$$-element expression $$A_1 A_2 \cdots A_n$$ where $$n \geq 2$$. By definition of full parenthesization, this must be the product of two fully parenthesized subexpressions enclosed in an outer pair of parentheses:

$$(P_{\text{left}})(P_{\text{right}})$$

where $$P_{\text{left}}$$ is a full parenthesization of $$A_1 \cdots A_k$$ (containing $$k$$ elements) and $$P_{\text{right}}$$ is a full parenthesization of $$A_{k+1} \cdots A_n$$ (containing $$n - k$$ elements), for some $$k$$ with $$1 \leq k < n$$.

By the inductive hypothesis:
- $$P_{\text{left}}$$ has exactly $$k - 1$$ pairs of parentheses
- $$P_{\text{right}}$$ has exactly $$(n - k) - 1 = n - k - 1$$ pairs of parentheses

The full parenthesization of the entire expression adds one more pair (the outermost parentheses enclosing the product):

$$\begin{align*}
\text{Total pairs} &= (k - 1) + (n - k - 1) + 1 \\
                   &= k - 1 + n - k - 1 + 1 \\
                   &= n - 1
\end{align*}$$

Therefore, a full parenthesization of an $$n$$-element expression has exactly $$n - 1$$ pairs of parentheses.

{% capture note %}
This result has a beautiful tree interpretation: a full parenthesization corresponds to a full binary tree with $$n$$ leaves (the matrices). Every full binary tree with $$n$$ leaves has exactly $$n - 1$$ internal nodes, and each internal node represents one multiplication operation that needs one pair of parentheses to delimit its operands. So $$n - 1$$ internal nodes means exactly $$n - 1$$ pairs of parentheses.
