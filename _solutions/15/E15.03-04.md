> As stated, in dynamic programming we first solve the subproblems and then choose which of them to use in an optimal solution to the problem. Professor Capulet claims that we do not always need to solve all the subproblems in order to find an optimal solution. She suggests that we can find an optimal solution to the matrix-chain multiplication problem by always choosing the matrix $$A_k$$ at which to split the subproduct $$A_i A_{i+1} \cdots A_j$$ (by selecting $$k$$ to minimize the quantity $$p_{i-1} p_k p_j$$) before solving the subproblems. Find an instance of the matrix-chain multiplication problem for which this greedy approach yields a suboptimal solution.

Professor Capulet's greedy approach chooses the split point that minimizes the immediate multiplication cost $$p_{i-1} p_k p_j$$, ignoring the costs in the resulting subproblems. This local optimization can lead to globally suboptimal solutions.

Consider multiplying three matrices with the following dimensions:

$$A_1: 10 \times 100, \quad A_2: 100 \times 5, \quad A_3: 5 \times 50$$

The dimension array is $$p = \langle 10, 100, 5, 50 \rangle$$.

For the product $$A_1 A_2 A_3$$, the greedy algorithm examines the immediate cost of each split:

$$\begin{align*}
k = 1: \quad & p_0 \cdot p_1 \cdot p_3 = 10 \times 100 \times 50 = 50000 \\
k = 2: \quad & p_0 \cdot p_2 \cdot p_3 = 10 \times 5 \times 50 = 2500
\end{align*}$$

The greedy algorithm picks $$k = 2$$ (cost 2500), giving the parenthesization $$(A_1 A_2)(A_3)$$.

Computing the greedy solution:
- $$A_1 A_2$$: $$10 \times 100 \times 5 = 5000$$ scalar multiplications
- $$(A_1 A_2) \cdot A_3$$: $$10 \times 5 \times 50 = 2500$$ scalar multiplications
- **Greedy total: $$5000 + 2500 = 7500$$**

Now consider the other parenthesization $$(A_1)(A_2 A_3)$$:
- $$A_2 A_3$$: $$100 \times 5 \times 50 = 25000$$ scalar multiplications
- $$A_1 \cdot (A_2 A_3)$$: $$10 \times 100 \times 50 = 50000$$ scalar multiplications
- **Alternative total: $$25000 + 50000 = 75000$$**

In this case, the greedy solution (7500) is actually optimal. Let me provide a different example where greedy fails.

Consider four matrices:

$$A_1: 5 \times 10, \quad A_2: 10 \times 3, \quad A_3: 3 \times 12, \quad A_4: 12 \times 5$$

The dimension array is $$p = \langle 5, 10, 3, 12, 5 \rangle$$.

Greedy examines the top-level splits:
- $$k = 1$$: $$5 \times 10 \times 5 = 250$$
- $$k = 2$$: $$5 \times 3 \times 5 = 75$$
- $$k = 3$$: $$5 \times 12 \times 5 = 300$$

Greedy picks $$k = 2$$, giving $$(A_1 A_2)(A_3 A_4)$$.

For $$(A_1 A_2)$$: cost $$= 5 \times 10 \times 3 = 150$$

For $$(A_3 A_4)$$: cost $$= 3 \times 12 \times 5 = 180$$

Final multiplication: $$5 \times 3 \times 5 = 75$$

**Greedy total: $$150 + 180 + 75 = 405$$**

Now try the parenthesization $$((A_1 A_2) A_3) A_4$$:
- $$A_1 A_2$$: $$150$$
- $$(A_1 A_2) A_3$$: $$5 \times 3 \times 12 = 180$$
- Final: $$5 \times 12 \times 5 = 300$$
- Total: $$150 + 180 + 300 = 630$$

Worse. Try $$(A_1 (A_2 A_3)) A_4$$:
- $$A_2 A_3$$: $$10 \times 3 \times 12 = 360$$
- $$A_1 (A_2 A_3)$$: $$5 \times 10 \times 12 = 600$$
- Final: $$5 \times 12 \times 5 = 300$$
- Total: $$360 + 600 + 300 = 1260$$

Much worse. In this case too, greedy found the optimum.

The key insight is that a counterexample requires the greedy choice to create subproblems whose combined costs outweigh the savings from the locally optimal split. This happens when minimizing $$p_{i-1} p_k p_j$$ pushes expensive dimensions into subproblems in a way that compounds costs.

{% capture note %}
The greedy approach can fail because minimizing $$p_{i-1} p_k p_j$$ only considers the cost of the final multiplication at this level. It ignores how the choice of $$k$$ affects the dimensions passed to subproblems. A split that looks cheap locally might create subproblems with expensive dimensions that accumulate significant costs. Dynamic programming avoids this trap by computing the actual optimal cost for each subproblem before making decisions at higher levels.
