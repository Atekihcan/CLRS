> Redo Exercise 17.1-3 using an accounting method of analysis.

Recall that Exercise 17.1-3 considers a sequence of $$n$$ operations on a data structure where the $$i$$th operation costs $$i$$ if $$i$$ is an exact power of 2, and costs 1 otherwise. We need to show that the amortized cost per operation is $$O(1)$$ using the accounting method.

Think of this like a subscription service that charges a flat rate but occasionally has "special events" (at powers of 2) that cost more. We want to show that by paying a constant fee each time, we can cover all costs including the expensive events.

Let us assign an amortized cost of 3 to each operation. We will show that this is sufficient to pay for all actual costs.

For operation $$i$$:

- If $$i$$ is **not** a power of 2: The actual cost is 1. We pay 1 from our amortized cost and save the remaining 2 as credit.

- If $$i$$ is a power of 2 (say $$i = 2^j$$): The actual cost is $$2^j$$. We need to show we've accumulated enough credit.

The key observation is that when we reach operation $$2^j$$, how much credit have we saved from operations since the previous power of 2?

Between operations $$2^{j-1} + 1$$ and $$2^j - 1$$, there are $$2^j - 2^{j-1} - 1 = 2^{j-1} - 1$$ non-power-of-2 operations. Each saves 2 credits, contributing $$2(2^{j-1} - 1) = 2^j - 2$$ credits.

{% include ads.html %}

At operation $$2^j$$, we also pay our amortized cost of 3. So we have:

- Credit saved: $$2^j - 2$$
- Current payment: 3
- Total available: $$2^j + 1$$

The actual cost of operation $$2^j$$ is exactly $$2^j$$, so we have $$2^j + 1 - 2^j = 1$$ credit left over.

To formalize this, let's track the credit more carefully. At any point after operation $$i$$:

$$\begin{align*}
\text{Total amortized paid} &= 3i \\
\text{Total actual cost} &= i + \sum_{j: 2^j \le i} (2^j - 1) \\
                         &= i + (2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} - 1) - (\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1) \\
                         &< i + 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} \\
                         &\le i + 2i = 3i
\end{align*}$$

Since the total amortized cost ($$3n$$) always exceeds the total actual cost, the credit remains nonnegative, confirming that an amortized cost of 3 per operation is sufficient.

{% capture note %}
The accounting method gives us flexibility in how we think about the problem. Instead of computing the exact total cost and dividing (as in aggregate analysis), we prepay for expensive operations during the cheap operations. Each "cheap" operation (cost 1) saves 2 credits toward the next "expensive" operation (power of 2).
{% endcapture %}
