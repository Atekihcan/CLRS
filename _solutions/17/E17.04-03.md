> Suppose that instead of contracting a table by halving its size when its load factor drops below $$1/4$$, we contract it by multiplying its size by $$2/3$$ when its load factor drops below $$1/3$$. Using the potential function
>
> $$\Phi(T) = \lvert 2 \cdot T.num - T.size \rvert$$
>
> show that the amortized cost of a $$\textsc{Table-Delete}$$ that uses this strategy is bounded above by a constant.

This alternative contraction scheme contracts more aggressively (at load factor $$1/3$$ instead of $$1/4$$) but by a smaller factor ($$2/3$$ instead of $$1/2$$). The potential function $$\Phi(T) = \lvert 2 \cdot num - size \rvert$$ is simpler than the piecewise function (17.6), using an absolute value to handle both cases uniformly.

The potential is zero when the load factor equals $$1/2$$ (i.e., when $$num = size/2$$). It grows as the load factor deviates from $$1/2$$ in either direction: reaching $$num$$ when the table is full ($$\alpha = 1$$) and reaching $$num$$ when $$\alpha = 1/3$$ (since $$size = 3 \cdot num$$ implies $$\lvert 2 \cdot num - 3 \cdot num \rvert = num$$).

After a deletion, we have $$num_i = num_{i-1} - 1$$. We need to analyze two cases: when the deletion triggers a contraction and when it does not.

**Case 1: No contraction ($$\alpha_i \ge 1/3$$)**

When no contraction occurs, $$size_i = size_{i-1}$$ and the actual cost is $$c_i = 1$$. We further split this into subcases based on whether $$2 \cdot num - size$$ is positive or negative.

**Subcase 1a:** $$\alpha_i \ge 1/2$$ (load factor at or above $$1/2$$)

Here $$2 \cdot num_i \ge size_i$$, so $$\Phi_i = 2 \cdot num_i - size_i$$. Similarly, $$\alpha_{i-1} > \alpha_i \ge 1/2$$ implies $$\Phi_{i-1} = 2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}$$. The amortized cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi_i - \Phi_{i-1} \\
&= 1 + (2 \cdot num_i - size_i) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + 2(num_{i-1} - 1) - size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} + size_{i-1} \\
&= 1 - 2 = -1
\end{align*}$$

**Subcase 1b:** $$1/3 \le \alpha_i < 1/2$$ (load factor between $$1/3$$ and $$1/2$$)

Here $$2 \cdot num_i < size_i$$, so $$\Phi_i = size_i - 2 \cdot num_i$$. If $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$, then $$\Phi_{i-1} = 2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}$$. The amortized cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= 1 + (size_i - 2 \cdot num_i) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + size_{i-1} - 2(num_{i-1} - 1) - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} + size_{i-1} \\
&= 1 + 2 \cdot size_{i-1} - 4 \cdot num_{i-1} + 2 \\
&= 3 + 2(size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1})
\end{align*}$$

Since $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$ means $$num_{i-1} \ge size_{i-1}/2$$, we have $$size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} \le 0$$. Thus $$\hat{c}_i \le 3$$.

If $$\alpha_{i-1} < 1/2$$, then both states are in the "below half" regime:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= 1 + (size_i - 2 \cdot num_i) - (size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + size_{i-1} - 2(num_{i-1} - 1) - size_{i-1} + 2 \cdot num_{i-1} \\
&= 1 + 2 = 3
\end{align*}$$

**Case 2: Contraction occurs ($$\alpha_i < 1/3$$ before deletion triggers contraction)**

When deletion causes the load factor to drop below $$1/3$$, we contract by multiplying the size by $$2/3$$. We have $$size_i = (2/3) \cdot size_{i-1}$$ and the actual cost is $$c_i = num_i + 1$$ (deleting one item and moving $$num_i$$ items to the new table).

Just before contraction, $$\alpha$$ dropped below $$1/3$$, meaning $$num_{i-1} - 1 < size_{i-1}/3$$ (after the deletion), so $$num_i = num_{i-1} - 1 < size_{i-1}/3$$. After contraction:

$$\alpha_i = \frac{num_i}{size_i} = \frac{num_i}{(2/3) \cdot size_{i-1}} = \frac{3 \cdot num_i}{2 \cdot size_{i-1}}$$

Since $$num_i < size_{i-1}/3$$, we have $$\alpha_i < 1/2$$. So after contraction, $$\Phi_i = size_i - 2 \cdot num_i$$.

Before the deletion (with $$\alpha_{i-1}$$ just above or at $$1/3$$), we had $$num_{i-1} \approx size_{i-1}/3$$, so $$\Phi_{i-1} = size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} \approx size_{i-1}/3 = num_{i-1}$$.

The amortized cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi_i - \Phi_{i-1} \\
&= (num_i + 1) + (size_i - 2 \cdot num_i) - (size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1}) \\
&= num_i + 1 + \frac{2}{3} size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_i - size_{i-1} + 2 \cdot num_{i-1} \\
&= num_i + 1 - \frac{1}{3} size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_i + 2(num_i + 1) \\
&= num_i + 1 - \frac{1}{3} size_{i-1} + 2 \\
&= num_i + 3 - \frac{1}{3} size_{i-1}
\end{align*}$$

At the contraction threshold, $$num_i \approx size_{i-1}/3 - 1$$, so:

$$\hat{c}_i \approx \frac{size_{i-1}}{3} - 1 + 3 - \frac{size_{i-1}}{3} = 2$$

{% capture note %}
In all cases, the amortized cost of $$\textsc{Table-Delete}$$ is bounded above by a small constant (at most 3). The potential function $$\Phi(T) = \lvert 2 \cdot num - size \rvert$$ elegantly captures how far the table is from its ideal load factor of $$1/2$$. When we contract at load factor $$1/3$$ and multiply size by $$2/3$$, the new load factor becomes $$\frac{(1/3)}{(2/3)} = \frac{1}{2}$$, returning the potential to zero. This is exactly the behavior we want: the potential builds up as the load factor deviates from $$1/2$$, and is released when a contraction brings it back to the ideal state.
{% endcapture %}
