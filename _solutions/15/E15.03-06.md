> Imagine that you wish to exchange one currency for another. You realize that instead of directly exchanging one currency for another, you might be better off making a series of trades through other currencies, winding up with the currency you want. Suppose that you can trade $$n$$ different currencies, numbered $$1, 2, \ldots, n$$, where you start with currency 1 and wish to wind up with currency $$n$$. You are given, for each pair of currencies $$i$$ and $$j$$, an exchange rate $$r_{ij}$$, meaning that if you start with $$d$$ units of currency $$i$$, you can trade for $$d \cdot r_{ij}$$ units of currency $$j$$. A sequence of trades may entail a commission, which depends on the number of trades you make. Let $$c_k$$ be the commission that you are charged when you make $$k$$ trades. Show that, if $$c_k = 0$$ for all $$k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$$, then the problem of finding the best sequence of exchanges from currency 1 to currency $$n$$ exhibits optimal substructure. Then show that if commissions $$c_k$$ are arbitrary values, then the problem of finding the best sequence of exchanges from currency 1 to currency $$n$$ does not necessarily exhibit optimal substructure.

### Part 1: Optimal Substructure with Zero Commissions

When all commissions are zero ($$c_k = 0$$ for all $$k$$), the problem has optimal substructure.

Consider an optimal sequence of exchanges from currency 1 to currency $$n$$:

$$1 \to i_1 \to i_2 \to \cdots \to i_{k-1} \to n$$

If we start with $$d$$ units of currency 1, we end with:

$$d \cdot r_{1,i_1} \cdot r_{i_1,i_2} \cdot \cdots \cdot r_{i_{k-1},n}$$

units of currency $$n$$ (with no commission deducted since all $$c_k = 0$$).

Suppose the intermediate sequence $$i_1 \to i_2 \to \cdots \to i_{k-1} \to n$$ is not optimal for going from currency $$i_1$$ to currency $$n$$. Then there exists a better sequence $$i_1 \to j_1 \to j_2 \to \cdots \to n$$ that yields more currency $$n$$ per unit of currency $$i_1$$. We could substitute this better sequence into our original path:

$$1 \to i_1 \to j_1 \to j_2 \to \cdots \to n$$

Since commissions are zero, the number of trades does not affect the final amount (no commission cost). The product of exchange rates would be strictly larger, contradicting our assumption that the original sequence was optimal.

The same cut-and-paste argument applies to any intermediate currency in the path. Therefore, optimal substructure holds: an optimal path from currency 1 to currency $$n$$ contains within it optimal subpaths.

### Part 2: No Optimal Substructure with Arbitrary Commissions

When commissions $$c_k$$ depend on the number of trades $$k$$, optimal substructure can fail. The key issue is that the number of trades used in one part of the solution affects the commission paid, which in turn affects whether a particular subpath is "optimal" for its portion.

Consider a simple example with 4 currencies and exchange rates:
- $$r_{1,2} = 2$$, $$r_{2,4} = 2$$ (path 1 $$\to$$ 2 $$\to$$ 4)
- $$r_{1,3} = 1.5$$, $$r_{3,4} = 4$$ (path 1 $$\to$$ 3 $$\to$$ 4)
- $$r_{1,4} = 3$$ (direct path)

Suppose commissions are:
- $$c_1 = 0$$ (one trade, no commission)
- $$c_2 = 2$$ (two trades, commission of 2 units from the final currency)
- $$c_3 = 10$$ (three trades, heavy commission)

Starting with $$d = 10$$ units of currency 1:

**Direct exchange (1 trade):** $$10 \cdot 3 - c_1 = 30 - 0 = 30$$ units of currency 4.

**Via currency 2 (2 trades):** $$10 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 - c_2 = 40 - 2 = 38$$ units of currency 4.

**Via currency 3 (2 trades):** $$10 \cdot 1.5 \cdot 4 - c_2 = 60 - 2 = 58$$ units of currency 4.

The optimal path is 1 $$\to$$ 3 $$\to$$ 4 with 58 units.

Now consider the subproblem: what is the optimal path from currency 3 to currency 4? Looking at just this subproblem in isolation:

**Direct (1 trade from 3):** Starting with $$x$$ units of currency 3, we get $$x \cdot 4 - c_1 = 4x$$ units of currency 4.

This is optimal for the subproblem, and indeed the overall solution uses this subpath. So far so good.

But here is where things break down. Suppose there were additional intermediate currencies between 3 and 4, and a path through them was cheaper when considered in isolation (as a 1-trade problem) but becomes expensive when used as part of a longer chain (where the trade count pushes us into a higher commission bracket).

More explicitly: if using a "locally optimal" 2-hop subpath from 3 to 4 would mean our total trade count becomes 3 instead of 2, the commission jumps from 2 to 10, potentially making the "locally optimal" subpath globally suboptimal.

The problem is that the "optimality" of a subpath depends on the context: how many trades have already been made. This is analogous to the longest simple path problem, where optimal subpaths depend on which vertices have already been used. Here, the "used resource" is the trade count, which affects the commission tier.

{% capture note %}
With arbitrary commissions, the optimal path from currency $$i$$ to currency $$n$$ depends on how many trades were already made to reach currency $$i$$. This contextual dependency means we cannot simply combine optimal subpath solutions. To solve this problem with arbitrary commissions, we would need to track the trade count as part of the state, expanding the subproblem space.
