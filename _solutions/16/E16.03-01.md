> Explain why, in the proof of Lemma 16.2, if $$x.\text{freq} = b.\text{freq}$$, then we must have $$a.\text{freq} = b.\text{freq} = x.\text{freq} = y.\text{freq}$$.

To understand why these frequencies must all be equal, let's carefully trace through the ordering constraints established in the proof of Lemma 16.2.

The proof establishes several ordering relationships. First, $$x$$ and $$y$$ are chosen as the two characters with the lowest frequencies in the alphabet $$C$$, and they are ordered so that $$x.\text{freq} \le y.\text{freq}$$. Second, $$a$$ and $$b$$ are two sibling leaves at maximum depth in an optimal tree $$T$$, and they are ordered so that $$a.\text{freq} \le b.\text{freq}$$.

From the fact that $$x$$ has the minimum frequency and $$a$$ is some character in the tree, we get:

$$x.\text{freq} \le a.\text{freq}$$

From the fact that $$y$$ has the second-lowest frequency (after $$x$$) and $$b$$ is distinct from $$x$$, we get:

$$y.\text{freq} \le b.\text{freq}$$

Now suppose $$x.\text{freq} = b.\text{freq}$$. We have the following chain of inequalities:

$$x.\text{freq} \le a.\text{freq} \le b.\text{freq} = x.\text{freq}$$

The first inequality ($$x.\text{freq} \le a.\text{freq}$$) holds because $$x$$ is a minimum-frequency character. The second inequality ($$a.\text{freq} \le b.\text{freq}$$) is by the ordering convention for $$a$$ and $$b$$. Since the leftmost and rightmost expressions are equal, every inequality in this chain must actually be an equality. Therefore, $$x.\text{freq} = a.\text{freq} = b.\text{freq}$$.

Similarly, from the ordering constraints we also have:

$$x.\text{freq} \le y.\text{freq} \le b.\text{freq} = x.\text{freq}$$

The first inequality holds by the ordering of $$x$$ and $$y$$, and the second holds because $$y$$ is the second-lowest frequency and $$b$$ is some character in the alphabet. Again, since the endpoints are equal, we must have $$x.\text{freq} = y.\text{freq}$$.

Combining these results:

$$a.\text{freq} = b.\text{freq} = x.\text{freq} = y.\text{freq}$$

{% capture note %}
When all four frequencies are equal, the lemma is trivially true. Any placement of $$x$$ and $$y$$ as siblings at maximum depth will work because swapping equal-frequency characters never changes the cost of the tree.
{% endcapture %}
