> Show that if $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$ and the $$i$$th operation on a dynamic table is $$\textsc{Table-Delete}$$, then the amortized cost of the operation with respect to the potential function (17.6) is bounded above by a constant.

The potential function (17.6) from the textbook is:

$$\Phi(T) = \begin{cases} 2 \cdot T.num - T.size & \text{if } \alpha(T) \ge 1/2 \\ T.size/2 - T.num & \text{if } \alpha(T) < 1/2 \end{cases}$$

We're given that before the $$i$$th operation, the load factor satisfies $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$. Since we're deleting an item, we have $$num_i = num_{i-1} - 1$$. The question is whether the table contracts and whether the load factor drops below $$1/2$$ after the deletion.

First, note that contraction only happens when the load factor drops below $$1/4$$. Since $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$, a single deletion cannot drop the load factor below $$1/4$$ (the most it can drop is slightly below $$1/2$$). Therefore, no contraction occurs, and we have $$size_i = size_{i-1}$$. The actual cost of the deletion is just $$c_i = 1$$.

We now need to consider two cases based on the load factor after the deletion.

**Case 1:** $$\alpha_i \ge 1/2$$ (load factor stays at or above $$1/2$$)

Both before and after the deletion, we use the first branch of the potential function. The amortized cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi_i - \Phi_{i-1} \\
&= 1 + (2 \cdot num_i - size_i) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + (2 \cdot (num_{i-1} - 1) - size_{i-1}) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + 2 \cdot num_{i-1} - 2 - size_{i-1} - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} + size_{i-1} \\
&= 1 - 2 \\
&= -1
\end{align*}$$

The amortized cost is $$-1$$, which means we actually release potential to help pay for future operations.

**Case 2:** $$\alpha_i < 1/2$$ (load factor drops below $$1/2$$)

Before the deletion, we use the first branch (since $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$), and after the deletion, we use the second branch (since $$\alpha_i < 1/2$$). The amortized cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi_i - \Phi_{i-1} \\
&= 1 + (size_i/2 - num_i) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + (size_{i-1}/2 - (num_{i-1} - 1)) - (2 \cdot num_{i-1} - size_{i-1}) \\
&= 1 + size_{i-1}/2 - num_{i-1} + 1 - 2 \cdot num_{i-1} + size_{i-1} \\
&= 2 + \frac{3}{2} size_{i-1} - 3 \cdot num_{i-1}
\end{align*}$$

Now we use the boundary conditions. Since $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$, we have $$num_{i-1} \ge size_{i-1}/2$$, which means $$size_{i-1} \le 2 \cdot num_{i-1}$$. Also, since $$\alpha_i < 1/2$$, we have $$num_i < size_i/2 = size_{i-1}/2$$, which gives us $$num_{i-1} - 1 < size_{i-1}/2$$, or $$num_{i-1} < size_{i-1}/2 + 1$$.

The load factor just dropped below $$1/2$$, so the values are very close to the boundary. At the exact boundary where $$\alpha_{i-1} = 1/2$$ (i.e., $$num_{i-1} = size_{i-1}/2$$):

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= 2 + \frac{3}{2} size_{i-1} - 3 \cdot \frac{size_{i-1}}{2} \\
&= 2 + \frac{3}{2} size_{i-1} - \frac{3}{2} size_{i-1} \\
&= 2
\end{align*}$$

When $$\alpha_{i-1} > 1/2$$, we have $$num_{i-1} > size_{i-1}/2$$, so $$3 \cdot num_{i-1} > \frac{3}{2} size_{i-1}$$, which makes the amortized cost smaller (more negative). Thus in all cases where $$\alpha_{i-1} \ge 1/2$$, the amortized cost is at most $$2$$.

{% capture note %}
In Case 1, the amortized cost is actually negative, meaning we're building up potential that can be used for future operations. In Case 2, we pay a constant amortized cost of at most 2 when transitioning from the "high load" regime to the "low load" regime of the potential function. Either way, the amortized cost is bounded by a constant.
