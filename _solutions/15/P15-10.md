> ***Planning an investment strategy***
>
> Your knowledge of algorithms helps you obtain an exciting job with the Acme Computer Company, along with a $10,000 signing bonus. You decide to invest this money with the goal of maximizing your return at the end of 10 years. You decide to use the Amalgamated Investment Company to manage your investments. Amalgamated Investments requires you to observe the following rules. It offers $$n$$ different investments, numbered 1 through $$n$$. In each year $$j$$, investment $$i$$ provides a return rate of $$r_{ij}$$. In other words, if you invest $$d$$ dollars in investment $$i$$ in year $$j$$, then at the end of year $$j$$, you have $$d \cdot r_{ij}$$ dollars. The return rates are guaranteed, that is, you are given all the return rates for the next 10 years for each investment. You make investment decisions only once per year. At the end of each year, you can leave the money made in the previous year in the same investments, or you can shift money to other investments, by either shifting money between existing investments or moving money to a new investment. If you do not move your money between two consecutive years, you pay a fee of $$f_1$$ dollars, whereas if you switch your money, you pay a fee of $$f_2$$ dollars, where $$f_2 > f_1$$.
>
> **a.** The problem, as stated, allows you to invest your money in multiple investments in each year. Prove that there exists an optimal investment strategy that, in each year, puts all the money into a single investment. (Recall that an optimal investment strategy maximizes the amount of money after 10 years and is not concerned with any other objectives, such as minimizing risk.)
>
> **b.** Prove that the problem of planning your optimal investment strategy exhibits optimal substructure.
>
> **c.** Design an algorithm that plans your optimal investment strategy. What is the running time of your algorithm?
>
> **d.** Suppose that Amalgamated Investments imposed the additional restriction that, at any point, you can have no more than $15,000 in any one investment. Show that the problem of maximizing your income at the end of 10 years no longer exhibits optimal substructure.

The core question here is: should we spread our money across multiple investments, or concentrate it in one? And once we know that, how do we pick which investment to use each year?

Think of it like choosing lanes in traffic. If lane A is faster than lane B this hour, putting half your cars in each lane doesn't make either half go faster. You might as well put everything in the fastest lane. The only complication is that switching lanes costs money.

### A

When return rates are linear (investing $$d$$ dollars gives $$d \cdot r_{ij}$$ return), there's no diversification benefit. The total return from splitting money is exactly the weighted average of individual returns.

Consider any year $$j$$ where we have money split across multiple investments. Let $$d_i$$ be the amount in investment $$i$$, with total $$D = \sum_i d_i$$. The return is:

$$\sum_i d_i \cdot r_{ij} = D \cdot \left( \sum_i \frac{d_i}{D} \cdot r_{ij} \right)$$

This weighted average cannot exceed the maximum return rate. If we let $$i^* = \arg\max_i r_{ij}$$, then putting all $$D$$ dollars in investment $$i^*$$ gives us $$D \cdot r_{i^*j} \geq D \cdot (\text{weighted average})$$.

The fee structure also supports concentration. With all money in one investment, we pay exactly one fee (either $$f_1$$ to stay or $$f_2$$ to switch). Splitting money across investments doesn't reduce fees and might increase them.

Therefore, for any strategy using multiple investments, we can construct a strategy using single investments each year that performs at least as well.

### B

Suppose we have an optimal investment strategy for years 1 through 10 that ends with all money in investment $$k$$ after year $$y$$, for any intermediate year $$y$$.

The strategy for years 1 through $$y$$ must itself be optimal among all strategies that end in investment $$k$$ after year $$y$$. Here's why: if we could do better in years 1 through $$y$$ while still ending in investment $$k$$, we'd have more money going into years $$y+1$$ through 10. Since the remaining years' strategy doesn't change, we'd end up with more money overall, contradicting optimality.

More formally, let $$M_y(i)$$ be the maximum money achievable after year $$y$$ ending in investment $$i$$. The optimal solution for years 1 through $$y+1$$ ending in investment $$j$$ is:

$$M_{y+1}(j) = \max_i \{ (M_y(i) - f_{i \to j}) \cdot r_{jy} \}$$

where $$f_{i \to j} = f_1$$ if $$i = j$$ (staying) and $$f_{i \to j} = f_2$$ if $$i \neq j$$ (switching).

This recurrence shows that solving year $$y+1$$ optimally requires knowing optimal solutions for year $$y$$. That's classic optimal substructure.

### C

We use dynamic programming with state $$M[y][i]$$ representing the maximum money after year $$y$$ when ending in investment $$i$$.

{% capture code %}
Optimal-Investment(r, n, years, f1, f2, initial)
    // M[y][i] = max money after year y ending in investment i
    let M[0..years, 1..n] be a new table
    let choice[0..years, 1..n] be a new table

    // Base case: year 1 (no prior investment, so "switch" cost applies)
    for i = 1 to n
        M[1][i] = (initial - f2) * r[i][1]
        choice[1][i] = 0    // no prior investment

    // Fill table year by year
    for y = 2 to years
        for j = 1 to n
            M[y][j] = -infinity
            for i = 1 to n
                if i == j
                    fee = f1    // staying in same investment
                else
                    fee = f2    // switching investments
                value = (M[y-1][i] - fee) * r[j][y]
                if value > M[y][j]
                    M[y][j] = value
                    choice[y][j] = i

    // Find best final investment
    best = 1
    for i = 2 to n
        if M[years][i] > M[years][best]
            best = i

    return M[years][best], choice
{% endcapture %}
{% include clrs_code.html title="Optimal-Investment(r, n, years, f1, f2, initial)" %}

**Running time analysis:** We have $$\text{years} \cdot n$$ states. For each state, we consider $$n$$ possible previous investments. Thus the running time is $$O(\text{years} \cdot n^2)$$. For the specific case of 10 years, this is $$O(10 \cdot n^2) = O(n^2)$$.

**Space:** $$O(\text{years} \cdot n)$$ for the tables, or $$O(n)$$ if we only keep the previous year's values.

### D

With a $15,000 cap on any single investment, the problem loses optimal substructure because optimal decisions for early years may depend on what happens in later years.

Consider this scenario:
- Initial investment: $10,000
- Investment A: returns 2x in year 1, returns 1.5x in year 2
- Investment B: returns 1x in year 1, returns 10x in year 2
- Fees are negligible

Without the cap, the greedy optimal strategy is clear: use A in year 1 (10K becomes 20K), then switch to B in year 2 (20K becomes 200K).

With the $15,000 cap:
- If we put all money in A year 1: 10K becomes 20K, but we can only keep 15K in any one investment for year 2. The excess 5K must go elsewhere.
- If we split 5K into A and 5K into B in year 1: After year 1 we have 10K (from A) + 5K (from B) = 15K. Now in year 2, we can put all 15K in B to get 150K.

But wait, consider: if we put all 10K in B in year 1, we still have 10K. In year 2, 10K in B becomes 100K, but the cap means we can't get more than 15K in B. The excess must go to A.

The key insight is that the optimal first-year allocation depends on the second-year returns. The decision about how to split money in year 1 cannot be made optimally without knowing what happens in year 2. This breaks the optimal substructure property because a locally optimal decision (maximize year 1 returns) may not lead to a globally optimal solution.

{% capture note %}
The $15,000 cap introduces a constraint that couples decisions across years. Without the cap, decisions are separable: we can maximize each year independently (subject to switch costs). With the cap, excess money must be redistributed, making the problem resemble a constrained optimization that doesn't decompose nicely.
{% endcapture %}
