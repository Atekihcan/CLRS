> Show that equation (15.4) follows from equation (15.3) and the initial condition $$T(0) = 1$$.

We are given the recurrence for the number of calls to $$\textsc{Cut-Rod}$$:

$$T(n) = 1 + \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} T(j)$$

with initial condition $$T(0) = 1$$. We want to prove that $$T(n) = 2^n$$.

The key insight is to recognize that this recurrence has a hidden pattern: each term in the sum represents all previous values of $$T$$, and the "1 +" accounts for the current call. This structure suggests the solution doubles with each increment of $$n$$.

Let's prove $$T(n) = 2^n$$ by strong induction.

**Base Case:** For $$n = 0$$, we have $$T(0) = 1 = 2^0$$. The base case holds.

**Inductive Hypothesis:** Assume $$T(k) = 2^k$$ for all $$k < n$$.

**Inductive Step:** We need to show $$T(n) = 2^n$$.

$$\begin{align*}
T(n) &= 1 + \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} T(j) \\
     &= 1 + \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} 2^j & \text{(by inductive hypothesis)} \\
     &= 1 + (2^0 + 2^1 + 2^2 + \cdots + 2^{n-1}) \\
     &= 1 + (2^n - 1) & \text{(geometric series sum)} \\
     &= 2^n
\end{align*}$$

Therefore, $$T(n) = 2^n$$ for all $$n \geq 0$$.

{% capture note %}
The geometric series formula $$\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} 2^j = 2^n - 1$$ is essential here. You can verify it: $$1 + 2 + 4 = 7 = 2^3 - 1$$, or $$1 + 2 + 4 + 8 = 15 = 2^4 - 1$$. This formula explains why the naive recursive rod-cutting algorithm has exponential running time, as it makes $$2^n$$ calls to solve a problem of size $$n$$.
