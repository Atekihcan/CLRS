> Show, by means of a counterexample, that the following "greedy" strategy does not always determine an optimal way to cut rods. Define the **density** of a rod of length $$i$$ to be $$p_i/i$$, that is, its value per inch. The greedy strategy for a rod of length $$n$$ cuts off a first piece of length $$i$$, where $$1 \le i \le n$$, having maximum density. It then continues by applying the greedy strategy to the remaining piece of length $$n - i$$.

The greedy approach sounds reasonable: always grab the piece with the best "bang for the buck" (highest value per inch). This is like a shopper who always buys the item with the best price-to-quality ratio. But in the rod-cutting problem, this local optimization can miss global optima.

Consider the standard price table from the textbook:

| Length $$i$$ | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|:------------:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| Price $$p_i$$ | 1 | 5 | 8 | 9 |
| Density $$p_i/i$$ | 1.00 | 2.50 | 2.67 | 2.25 |

For a rod of length $$n = 4$$, the greedy algorithm proceeds as follows:

1. Compare densities: length 3 has the highest density (2.67), so cut off a piece of length 3
2. This leaves a piece of length 1
3. The piece of length 1 has density 1.00, so we take it as-is

The greedy solution yields revenue: $$p_3 + p_1 = 8 + 1 = 9$$

However, the optimal solution is to cut the rod into two pieces of length 2:

$$p_2 + p_2 = 5 + 5 = 10$$

The greedy algorithm returns 9, but the optimal revenue is 10.

{% capture note %}
The greedy approach fails because maximizing local density does not account for how well the remaining piece can be utilized. When we greedily take the length-3 piece, we're left with a length-1 piece that has terrible value. Two length-2 pieces together have slightly lower average density than a length-3 piece, but they leave no wasted or undervalued remainder. This is a classic example of why dynamic programming is needed: we must consider all possible decompositions, not just locally optimal choices.
