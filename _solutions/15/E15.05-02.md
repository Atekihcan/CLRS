> Determine the cost and structure of an optimal binary search tree for a set of $$n = 7$$ keys with the following probabilities:
>
> | $$i$$   | 0    | 1    | 2    | 3    | 4    | 5    | 6    | 7    |
> |---------|------|------|------|------|------|------|------|------|
> | $$p_i$$ |      | 0.04 | 0.06 | 0.08 | 0.02 | 0.10 | 0.12 | 0.14 |
> | $$q_i$$ | 0.06 | 0.06 | 0.06 | 0.06 | 0.05 | 0.05 | 0.05 | 0.05 |

Let's apply the $$\textsc{Optimal-BST}$$ algorithm from the chapter. We need to build the tables $$e[i, j]$$, $$w[i, j]$$, and $$root[i, j]$$ step by step. The table $$e[i, j]$$ stores the expected search cost for the optimal subtree containing keys $$k_i, \ldots, k_j$$, while $$w[i, j]$$ stores the sum of all probabilities in that subtree.

First, let's verify that the probabilities sum to 1:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{7} p_i + \sum_{i=0}^{7} q_i = (0.04 + 0.06 + 0.08 + 0.02 + 0.10 + 0.12 + 0.14) + (0.06 + 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.05 + 0.05 + 0.05 + 0.05) = 0.56 + 0.44 = 1.00$$

**Initialization (Base Cases):** For $$i = 1, 2, \ldots, 8$$:

$$e[i, i-1] = q_{i-1} \quad \text{and} \quad w[i, i-1] = q_{i-1}$$

This gives us:
- $$e[1, 0] = w[1, 0] = q_0 = 0.06$$
- $$e[2, 1] = w[2, 1] = q_1 = 0.06$$
- $$e[3, 2] = w[3, 2] = q_2 = 0.06$$
- $$e[4, 3] = w[4, 3] = q_3 = 0.06$$
- $$e[5, 4] = w[5, 4] = q_4 = 0.05$$
- $$e[6, 5] = w[6, 5] = q_5 = 0.05$$
- $$e[7, 6] = w[7, 6] = q_6 = 0.05$$
- $$e[8, 7] = w[8, 7] = q_7 = 0.05$$

**Computing for length $$l = 1$$ (single keys):**

For each key $$k_i$$ alone, we compute $$w[i, i] = w[i, i-1] + p_i + q_i$$ and $$e[i, i] = e[i, i-1] + e[i+1, i] + w[i, i]$$:

$$\begin{align*}
w[1,1] &= 0.06 + 0.04 + 0.06 = 0.16, & e[1,1] &= 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.16 = 0.28, & root[1,1] &= 1 \\
w[2,2] &= 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.06 = 0.18, & e[2,2] &= 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.18 = 0.30, & root[2,2] &= 2 \\
w[3,3] &= 0.06 + 0.08 + 0.06 = 0.20, & e[3,3] &= 0.06 + 0.06 + 0.20 = 0.32, & root[3,3] &= 3 \\
w[4,4] &= 0.06 + 0.02 + 0.05 = 0.13, & e[4,4] &= 0.06 + 0.05 + 0.13 = 0.24, & root[4,4] &= 4 \\
w[5,5] &= 0.05 + 0.10 + 0.05 = 0.20, & e[5,5] &= 0.05 + 0.05 + 0.20 = 0.30, & root[5,5] &= 5 \\
w[6,6] &= 0.05 + 0.12 + 0.05 = 0.22, & e[6,6] &= 0.05 + 0.05 + 0.22 = 0.32, & root[6,6] &= 6 \\
w[7,7] &= 0.05 + 0.14 + 0.05 = 0.24, & e[7,7] &= 0.05 + 0.05 + 0.24 = 0.34, & root[7,7] &= 7
\end{align*}$$

**Computing for length $$l = 2$$:**

For $$e[i, j]$$, we try each possible root $$r \in \{i, \ldots, j\}$$ and pick the one minimizing $$e[i, r-1] + e[r+1, j] + w[i, j]$$.

$$\begin{align*}
w[1,2] &= 0.16 + 0.06 + 0.06 = 0.28 \\
e[1,2] &= \min(e[1,0] + e[2,2], e[1,1] + e[3,2]) + w[1,2] \\
       &= \min(0.06 + 0.30, 0.28 + 0.06) + 0.28 = \min(0.36, 0.34) + 0.28 = 0.62, \quad root[1,2] = 2
\end{align*}$$

Continuing this process for all pairs:

$$\begin{align*}
w[2,3] &= 0.38, & e[2,3] &= 0.68, & root[2,3] &= 3 \\
w[3,4] &= 0.27, & e[3,4] &= 0.50, & root[3,4] &= 3 \\
w[4,5] &= 0.28, & e[4,5] &= 0.48, & root[4,5] &= 5 \\
w[5,6] &= 0.37, & e[5,6] &= 0.57, & root[5,6] &= 6 \\
w[6,7] &= 0.41, & e[6,7] &= 0.61, & root[6,7] &= 7
\end{align*}$$

**Continuing for lengths $$l = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7$$:**

**Final $$e$$ and $$root$$ tables:**

**The optimal expected search cost is $$e[1, 7] = 3.12$$.**

**The structure of the optimal BST:**

Using $$root[1, 7] = 5$$ as a starting point, we can reconstruct the tree:

- $$k_5$$ is the root
- Left subtree (keys $$k_1, \ldots, k_4$$): $$root[1, 4] = 2$$, so $$k_2$$ is left child of $$k_5$$
  - $$root[1, 1] = 1$$: $$k_1$$ is left child of $$k_2$$
    - $$d_0$$ is left child of $$k_1$$, $$d_1$$ is right child of $$k_1$$
  - $$root[3, 4] = 3$$: $$k_3$$ is right child of $$k_2$$
    - $$d_2$$ is left child of $$k_3$$
    - $$root[4, 4] = 4$$: $$k_4$$ is right child of $$k_3$$
      - $$d_3$$ is left child of $$k_4$$, $$d_4$$ is right child of $$k_4$$
- Right subtree (keys $$k_6, k_7$$): $$root[6, 7] = 7$$, so $$k_7$$ is right child of $$k_5$$
  - $$root[6, 6] = 6$$: $$k_6$$ is left child of $$k_7$$
    - $$d_5$$ is left child of $$k_6$$, $$d_6$$ is right child of $$k_6$$
  - $$d_7$$ is right child of $$k_7$$

{% capture note %}
Notice that $$k_7$$ has the highest probability ($$p_7 = 0.14$$), yet it is not the root. The algorithm found that $$k_5$$ as the root gives better overall expected cost because it better balances the tree considering all the key and dummy key probabilities. This illustrates why a greedy approach of always choosing the highest-probability key as root would fail.
