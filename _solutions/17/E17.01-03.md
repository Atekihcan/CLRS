> Suppose we perform a sequence of $$n$$ operations on a data structure in which the $$i$$th operation costs $$i$$ if $$i$$ is an exact power of 2, and 1 otherwise. Use aggregate analysis to determine the amortized cost per operation.

This is a classic example where aggregate analysis reveals that occasional expensive operations are "paid for" by the many cheap operations surrounding them. Think of it like a subscription service where you pay a small amount each month, but every year there is a larger annual fee. The question is: what is the average monthly cost?

The cost of operation $$i$$ is:

$$c_i = \begin{cases} i & \text{if } i = 2^j \text{ for some integer } j \ge 0 \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

To find the total cost of $$n$$ operations, we split it into two parts: the cost from expensive operations (powers of 2) and the cost from cheap operations (everything else).

The powers of 2 that are at most $$n$$ are $$1, 2, 4, 8, \ldots, 2^{\lfloor \lg n \rfloor}$$. The sum of these is:

$$\sum_{j=0}^{\lfloor \lg n \rfloor} 2^j = 2^{\lfloor \lg n \rfloor + 1} - 1 < 2n$$

For the remaining operations (those where $$i$$ is not a power of 2), each costs 1. There are at most $$n$$ such operations, contributing at most $$n$$ to the total cost.

{% include ads.html %}

Combining both parts, the total cost is:

$$\begin{align*}
T(n) &= \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i \\
     &= \sum_{j=0}^{\lfloor \lg n \rfloor} 2^j + (\text{number of non-powers of 2}) \cdot 1 \\
     &< 2n + n \\
     &= 3n
\end{align*}$$

Therefore, the total cost of $$n$$ operations is $$O(n)$$, and the amortized cost per operation is:

$$\frac{T(n)}{n} < \frac{3n}{n} = 3 = O(1)$$

{% capture note %}
The geometric series is the key insight here. Even though operation $$2^j$$ costs $$2^j$$ (which could be as large as $$n/2$$ for $$j = \lg n - 1$$), the sum of all powers of 2 up to $$n$$ is less than $$2n$$. This is because powers of 2 are spaced exponentially apart: there are only $$\lg n$$ expensive operations among the $$n$$ total operations. This "exponential spacing" of expensive operations is a common pattern in data structures, appearing in dynamic arrays, binary counters, and splay trees.
