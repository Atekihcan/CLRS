> Redo Exercise 17.1-3 using a potential method of analysis.

Recall from Exercise 17.1-3: we perform a sequence of $$n$$ operations on a data structure in which the $$i$$th operation costs $$i$$ if $$i$$ is an exact power of 2, and costs 1 otherwise. We want to show that the amortized cost per operation is $$O(1)$$.

The trick with the potential method is to design a potential function that "stores up" credit during cheap operations and releases it to pay for expensive ones. Think of it like a savings account: we deposit a little extra during normal operations so we can afford the occasional big expense.

Define the potential function after the $$i$$th operation as:

$$\Phi(D_i) = 2i - 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1}$$

For $$i = 0$$, we define $$\Phi(D_0) = 0$$.

To understand this choice, note that $$2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1}$$ is the smallest power of 2 greater than $$i$$. The potential represents how far we are from the next expensive operation (which happens at a power of 2) scaled by 2.

Let us compute the amortized cost for both cases:

**Case 1: $$i$$ is not a power of 2** (cost is 1)

When $$i$$ is not a power of 2, we have $$\lfloor \lg i \rfloor = \lfloor \lg(i-1) \rfloor$$, so the threshold $$2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1}$$ remains unchanged.

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi(D_i) - \Phi(D_{i-1}) \\
          &= 1 + (2i - 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1}) - (2(i-1) - 2^{\lfloor \lg(i-1) \rfloor + 1}) \\
          &= 1 + 2i - 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} - 2i + 2 + 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} \\
          &= 3
\end{align*}$$

**Case 2: $$i = 2^k$$ for some $$k \ge 0$$ (cost is $$i$$)

When $$i$$ is a power of 2, say $$i = 2^k$$, we have $$\lfloor \lg i \rfloor = k$$, so $$2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} = 2^{k+1} = 2i$$.

For $$i - 1 = 2^k - 1$$, we have $$\lfloor \lg(i-1) \rfloor = k - 1$$, so $$2^{\lfloor \lg(i-1) \rfloor + 1} = 2^k = i$$.

$$\begin{align*}
\hat{c}_i &= c_i + \Phi(D_i) - \Phi(D_{i-1}) \\
          &= i + (2i - 2i) - (2(i-1) - i) \\
          &= i + 0 - (2i - 2 - i) \\
          &= i - i + 2 \\
          &= 2
\end{align*}$$

Finally, we verify that $$\Phi(D_i) \ge 0$$ for all $$i \ge 1$$. Since $$2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor} \le i < 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1}$$, we have:

$$\Phi(D_i) = 2i - 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} \ge 2 \cdot 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor} - 2^{\lfloor \lg i \rfloor + 1} = 0$$

Since the amortized cost is at most 3 for every operation and $$\Phi(D_n) \ge \Phi(D_0) = 0$$, the total actual cost is at most $$3n$$. Therefore, the amortized cost per operation is $$O(1)$$.
