> Find an optimal parenthesization of a matrix-chain product whose sequence of dimensions is $$\langle 5, 10, 3, 12, 5, 50, 6 \rangle$$.

The dimension sequence $$\langle 5, 10, 3, 12, 5, 50, 6 \rangle$$ defines 6 matrices: $$A_1$$ is $$5 \times 10$$, $$A_2$$ is $$10 \times 3$$, $$A_3$$ is $$3 \times 12$$, $$A_4$$ is $$12 \times 5$$, $$A_5$$ is $$5 \times 50$$, and $$A_6$$ is $$50 \times 6$$.

Think of it like planning a factory assembly line: the order in which you combine intermediate products can dramatically affect total manufacturing cost. Some matrices are "cheap" to multiply (when they share small dimensions), while others are expensive.

Running the $$\textsc{Matrix-Chain-Order}$$ algorithm, we build up the $$m$$ and $$s$$ tables. The $$m[i,j]$$ entry stores the minimum number of scalar multiplications needed to compute $$A_{i..j}$$, and $$s[i,j]$$ stores the optimal split point.

The tables are filled in order of increasing chain length $$l = j - i + 1$$:

**Chain length 1** (diagonal): $$m[i,i] = 0$$ for all $$i$$.

**Chain length 2**:
$$\begin{align*}
m[1,2] &= p_0 \cdot p_1 \cdot p_2 = 5 \cdot 10 \cdot 3 = 150 \\
m[2,3] &= p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot p_3 = 10 \cdot 3 \cdot 12 = 360 \\
m[3,4] &= p_2 \cdot p_3 \cdot p_4 = 3 \cdot 12 \cdot 5 = 180 \\
m[4,5] &= p_3 \cdot p_4 \cdot p_5 = 12 \cdot 5 \cdot 50 = 3000 \\
m[5,6] &= p_4 \cdot p_5 \cdot p_6 = 5 \cdot 50 \cdot 6 = 1500
\end{align*}$$

**Chain length 3**:
$$\begin{align*}
m[1,3] &= \min(m[1,1] + m[2,3] + 5 \cdot 10 \cdot 12,\ m[1,2] + m[3,3] + 5 \cdot 3 \cdot 12) \\
       &= \min(0 + 360 + 600,\ 150 + 0 + 180) = \min(960, 330) = 330 \\
m[2,4] &= \min(m[2,2] + m[3,4] + 10 \cdot 3 \cdot 5,\ m[2,3] + m[4,4] + 10 \cdot 12 \cdot 5) \\
       &= \min(0 + 180 + 150,\ 360 + 0 + 600) = \min(330, 960) = 330 \\
m[3,5] &= \min(m[3,3] + m[4,5] + 3 \cdot 12 \cdot 50,\ m[3,4] + m[5,5] + 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 50) \\
       &= \min(0 + 3000 + 1800,\ 180 + 0 + 750) = \min(4800, 930) = 930 \\
m[4,6] &= \min(m[4,4] + m[5,6] + 12 \cdot 5 \cdot 6,\ m[4,5] + m[6,6] + 12 \cdot 50 \cdot 6) \\
       &= \min(0 + 1500 + 360,\ 3000 + 0 + 3600) = \min(1860, 6600) = 1860
\end{align*}$$

Continuing this process for chain lengths 4, 5, and 6:

**Chain length 4**:
$$\begin{align*}
m[1,4] &= 330 + 180 + 5 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 = 585 \quad (k = 2) \\
m[2,5] &= 330 + 750 + 10 \cdot 5 \cdot 50 = 3580 \quad (k = 3) \\
m[3,6] &= 930 + 1500 + 3 \cdot 50 \cdot 6 = 3330 \quad (k = 5)
\end{align*}$$

**Chain length 5**:
$$\begin{align*}
m[1,5] &= 585 + 750 + 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 50 = 2585 \quad (k = 4) \\
m[2,6] &= 330 + 1860 + 10 \cdot 5 \cdot 6 = 2490 \quad (k = 3)
\end{align*}$$

**Chain length 6**:
$$\begin{align*}
m[1,6] &= \min_{1 \le k < 6} \{ m[1,k] + m[k+1,6] + p_0 p_k p_6 \} \\
       &= 2585 + 1500 + 5 \cdot 50 \cdot 6 = 5585 \quad (k = 5)
\end{align*}$$

The $$s$$ table records the optimal split points:

$$s[1,6] = 5, s[1,5] = 4, s[1,4] = 2, s[1,2] = 1, s[5,6] = 5, s[3,4] = 3, s[4,5] = 4$$

Following the $$s$$ table recursively from $$s[1,6] = 5$$:
- Split $$A_1 \ldots A_6$$ between $$A_5$$ and $$A_6$$
- For $$A_1 \ldots A_5$$: split at $$s[1,5] = 4$$
- For $$A_1 \ldots A_4$$: split at $$s[1,4] = 2$$
- And so on...

The optimal parenthesization is:

$$((A_1 A_2)(A_3 A_4))(A_5 A_6)$$

This requires **5585** scalar multiplications.
